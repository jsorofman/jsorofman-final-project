Healthcare Learning Management System (LMS):
New Information Architecture

Background

This healthcare learning management system (LMS) is marketed as a strategic solution to vastly improve patient outcomes in healthcare facilities. By delivering sophisticated online assessments and training, healthcare companies can identify employee skills gaps and provide required remediation.

The Design Challenge

The global navigation in the product looked like this:

  • Nothing about the information architecture communicated strategic value.
  • Links in the highest level of the left-hand navigation covered a broad range of content types, some which were content objects and others were functionality. The purpose of those objects and their relationships to other links were unclear.
  • According to user surveys, the navigation was cluttered, confusing and didn't link to functionality that should be easily accessible. It took a lot of time to onboard new users since none of it was intuitive.

The navigation was a huge missed revenue opportunity. It failed to provide a content structure that supported product and packaging strategies by highlighting new product offerings and showing end-to-end solutions. Drawing the connection between information architecture and business goals was important when advocating this initiative as a priority to product leadership.

Goals

I defined the information architecture goals from both user and business perspectives:

  • Create a user experience for LMS clients that integrates seamlessly into their daily workflow.
  • Provide direct and intuitive paths for users to efficiently complete the tasks they set out to do.
  • Speak to users in their language.
  • Organize information and design functionality according to their view of the world.
  • Develop a flexible, scalable framework that can easily accommodate current and future LMS product offerings.
  • Design a site structure in which new initiatives can be packaged and highlighted to clients instead of lost in the UI.

Initial Exploration

While the current site structure happened as a function of aggregating similar content types, I took a task-oriented approach to improving it.

"What do users want to accomplish when coming to the platform?"

According to user research, the list of tasks included:

  • Assign training
  • Create training
  • Assess skills and competencies
  • Run reports
  • Check organizational compliance
  • Manage users in the system

Initial Solution

I took an initial shot at creating a sitemap that focused on the tasks listed above. It broke the top level IA out into major task areas and spec’d out which existing content types would fall into each category, like a hybrid sitemap/content inventory.

“Users” became “People,” and the different kinds of users - administrators, learners and applicants - were delineated separately in the next level of the IA. Administrators manage users within the system; learners are assigned training.

It’s also important to distinguish a job applicant being assessed for a position from a full-time employee with a more permanent status in the system.Each user type has different tasks associated with it, and when they were lumped under the broad category of “Users,” it was hard to find the right people in the system and the right tasks.

Because this LMS is a mission-driven product to improve healthcare outcomes for people, the use of the word “People” at the highest level of navigation supports that brand standard and serves as a reminder that people are at the heart of this effort.

“Learning” stayed the same, but the subnavigation became “Create Training” and “Assign Training,” creating a clear path for the tasks that happen within that topic. All the content linked under this category in the previous navigation will get rolled up in the UI and appear as tasks are being completed.

Diving a bit deeper, “Training” was a big, all-encompassing category that wasn’t limited to just training. There were content objects like skills checklists and competency assessments that provided a mechanism for evaluating learners’ skills and abilities, which isn’t the same as training.

For the purposes of this exercise, I defined “Training” as “acquiring new information,” and differentiated the skills checklists and assessments from training as learners “demonstrating what they know.”I created a separate category for that content to support that distinction: “Evaluations.”

“Reporting” was self-explanatory and remained the same.

Validation, Round 1

Unfortunately, the timing around initial validation came at the start of a global pandemic, and our target users are healthcare administrators. It wasn’t an appropriate time to reach out to them for validation. I took the sitemap to my next best audience: Internal client success managers (CSMs). They are the bridge between our product and end users, and they have encyclopedic knowledge of client types, use cases, and the gaps where the product falls short on usability to the point where clients pick up the phone for help.

I’d grouped our existing content into task-oriented categories and I wanted unbiased feedback as to what those categories should be. I deleted the labels of the highest level categories from their boxes on the sitemap. Instead of asking the CSMs whether my interpretation of those categories made sense, I asked them what labels they would assign if given the IA job. I got invaluable feedback around both the content groupings and CSM insights into what they would call those groups. Validation, Round 2

After the initial response to the pandemic, our clients indicated they were available again to provide feedback. I chose a hosted card sort activity using Optimal Workshop as the most convenient and least intrusive way for them to do that. I set up the activity and asked platform administrators across client verticals to determine where content objects fit into major navigation categories. Surprisingly, the response was significantly higher for this activity than it had been for previous UX surveys – perhaps the sorting activity was a welcome diversion at work?

We analyzed the data from 126 completed card sorts and determined that the categories were intuitive and clear about nearly every piece of major functionality on the platform. There was one exception where participants placed the “Hierarchies” functionality in a 50/50 split between “People” and “Site Settings.”

Because Hierarchies is an important tool that enables healthcare administrators to organize training groups and hold supervisors accountable for compliance, this split generated an additional user research initiative to better understand how to improve the functionality and how to define it in terms of the site IA. For the purpose of this project, the research put the functionality squarely in the “Users” category.

Final Solution

Once the revisions were made as a result of internal CSM and client validation, I met with the director of product management to review the product roadmap and play a game of “IA Jenga” where the goal was to determine if any roadmap initiatives would collapse the site structure. I was pleased to report that the site structure was flexible enough to incorporate all new product features for the next two years of development.

The final IA is reflected in the wireframe below. When you compare the full left hand navigation in this wireframe to the navigation bars at the top of this case study, the impact of the redesign is clear. It provides users a direct and unambiguous path to getting work done. It brings new product initiatives and content to the surface. It eliminates confusion and redundancies. The streamlined nature of the navigation supports the simplicity of the new brand and visual redesign that will be rolling out as part of this effort.

Key Outcomes

The content I previously categorized as “Evaluations” evolved into a new content category called “Competency Management.” While competency management was a product initiative in the works, it had not been clearly defined or considered relative to the information architecture in the LMS. It had also been obscured as a form of “training,” so this design process was instrumental in clearly defining a new product with significant future revenue streams.

Information architecture in general was a new concept to this product organization, and this exercise demonstrated the value of this approach to product design.In less than a year since I initiated this effort, the final site IA has become the “North Star” for a holistic product redesign, as well as the foundation for a new services-based backend architecture and the backbone of a new pricing and packaging strategy. This initiative quickly gained enterprise-wide visibility due to its significant impact on product strategy and direction.
